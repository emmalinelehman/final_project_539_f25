Arctic Breeding Grounds

The journey begins under the endless summer sun of the Arctic tundra. Here, amidst blooming wildflowers and rocky outcrops, the terns court, nest, and raise their young in the safety of the 24-hour daylight. It is a time of abundance, preparing the next generation for the monumental flight ahead.

North Atlantic Departure

As the polar light fades and the days grow shorter, the terns lift from their nesting grounds. They gather in great flocks, their calls echoing against the cliffs, before embarking on their southward flight across the turbulent North Atlantic, leaving the solid earth behind for the open sky.

Mid-Atlantic Crossing

Over deep blue waters, the Arctic Tern travels thousands of miles—one of the longest continuous flights of any creature on Earth. They glide effortlessly on ocean breezes, dipping to the surface to snatch small fish, barely expending energy as they traverse the vast, featureless horizon.

Southern Ocean Passage

Guided by ancient instincts, ocean currents, and trade winds, the terns navigate the roaring forties and furious fifties. They glide along the edges of continents, using the powerful westerlies to propel them toward the icy, nutrient-rich waters of the southern seas.

Antarctic Feeding Grounds

Endless daylight returns as the terns reach the Antarctic pack ice. Here, they molt and feast on krill in the cold violet glow of the southern summer, enjoying a second summer in a single year—the only animal on Earth to see more daylight than any other.

Return North

As the Antarctic winter approaches, the Arctic Tern traces its S-shaped path north again. Battling headwinds and storms, they complete a staggering 44,000-mile round trip, returning to the Arctic just as the snow melts, ready to begin the cycle of life anew.
